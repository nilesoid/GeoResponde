# Vision

> **An open humanitarian ecosystem where independent organizations collaborate without sacrificing their autonomy.**

*Connecting humanitarian information, not replacing it.*

---

# Why GeoResponde Exists

GeoResponde was born from a simple but critical observation during emergency response operations:

**Information fragmentation costs time, coordination, and ultimately lives.**

When disasters strike, dozens of independent initiatives emerge almost immediately. Volunteers publish missing persons lists. NGOs open shelters. Hospitals report their operational status. Communities create WhatsApp groups. Governments release official updates. Researchers publish scientific analyses.

Every initiative is valuable.

The problem is that they rarely communicate with one another.

At the same time, scientific institutions generate highly valuable information—earthquake locations, fault models, satellite imagery, ground deformation, weather forecasts, flood predictions and many other datasets. Yet this scientific intelligence often remains disconnected from humanitarian operations on the ground.

**GeoResponde exists to bridge these worlds.**

Rather than replacing existing efforts, GeoResponde connects them.

---

# Mission

Enable humanitarian organizations, scientific institutions, volunteers and affected communities to collaborate more effectively by making critical information easier to discover, understand and share.

GeoResponde seeks to reduce fragmentation, increase transparency, and improve access to trustworthy information during emergencies.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Federation over Centralization

GeoResponde does not seek to own humanitarian information.

Original organizations should remain the authoritative source for the information they publish.

Our role is to connect existing systems, not replace them.

---

## Open by Default

GeoResponde is developed as an open-source project.

Architecture discussions, documentation and source code are public whenever possible.

Transparency encourages collaboration and builds trust.

---

## Interoperability First

Every integration should move the humanitarian ecosystem closer to interoperability rather than tighter coupling.

Whenever possible, GeoResponde adopts and builds upon existing open standards instead of reinventing them.

---

## Scientific Intelligence Meets Humanitarian Operations

Effective emergency response requires both macro-level understanding and micro-level action.

Knowing that a hospital is operational is valuable.

Knowing that the same hospital is located within an area affected by severe ground shaking, landslide risk or flooding can fundamentally change operational decisions.

GeoResponde aims to bring scientific intelligence and humanitarian information together within a common operational picture.

---

## Human-Centered Design

Technology should reduce uncertainty, not increase it.

Interfaces should remain understandable by responders, volunteers, researchers and affected communities alike.

---

# Roadmap

GeoResponde is envisioned as a long-term evolution rather than a fixed product.

## Stage 1 — Federation

Connect existing humanitarian and scientific information providers through adapters.

Goals include:

- Federated Search
- Federated Reporting
- Scientific Situation Room
- Provider Gateway
- Provider Registry

---

## Stage 2 — Interoperability

Reduce the need for custom integrations.

Investigate and adopt existing interoperability standards wherever possible.

Examples include:

- CAP
- HXL
- PFIF
- EDXL
- STAC
- GeoJSON

The objective is to enable organizations to communicate through shared interfaces instead of bespoke integrations.

---

## Stage 3 — Open Humanitarian Interface (OHI)

If existing standards do not fully address interoperability needs, GeoResponde will work with the community to define an open interface for humanitarian collaboration.

The goal is **not** to replace existing standards.

Instead, OHI should compose, extend and simplify them where necessary.

Potential capabilities include:

- Federated Search
- Federated Reporting
- Provider Discovery
- Capability Negotiation
- Shared Metadata
- Authentication Profiles

The specification should remain open, community-driven and implementation-independent.

---

## Stage 4 — Ecosystem

GeoResponde should eventually become one participant within a broader humanitarian ecosystem.

Success is not measured by the number of organizations using GeoResponde itself.

Success is measured by the number of independent systems capable of collaborating through open standards while maintaining their own autonomy.

---

# Definition of Success

GeoResponde succeeds when:

- Information becomes easier to discover.
- Scientific and humanitarian communities collaborate more effectively.
- Organizations remain independent while becoming better connected.
- Open standards reduce duplicated effort across the humanitarian ecosystem.
- Communities receive faster, clearer and more trustworthy information during emergencies.

---

# Beyond GeoResponde

GeoResponde is not the destination.

It is one step toward a more open humanitarian ecosystem.

If one day humanitarian organizations can seamlessly interoperate through shared open standards without depending on GeoResponde itself, we will consider that a success.

Our success will not be measured by how much information GeoResponde owns, but by how much information it helps connect.
